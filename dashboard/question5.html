Question 5

Is there a statistically significant difference between the academic performance of lower income households VS non-lower income households due to COVID-19?

Time Spent Learning for Low-Income Students VS Non Low-Income Students, before VS during COVID-19.
Method: Welch Two Sample T-Test
  • To compare the results from question 3 and 4, we used a Welch Two Sample T-Test. We conducted a two-sided test in order to cover all possibilities. The data analysis was done in R.
  • Our null hypothesis was that there was no difference in academic performance between students of lower and non-lower income households, both before VS during COVID-19.
  • We assumed the variances to be unequal because we used data from two different groups and did not know if our variances are the same or not. For both t-tests, we operated under a 95% as confidence interval.
  • By the results of this two-sided t-test, we were able to use the p-value to conclude whether there is a statistically significant difference between the academic performance of students from lower income households VS the non-lower income households.
Demo: Welch Two Sample T-Test

T-Test #1: Before COVID-19

For the first t-test performed, we compared whether there was statistically significant difference between the learning times (hours per week) for students from non low-income families VS students from low-income families prior to COVID-19. The resulting p-value from this t-test was much less than the 0.05 threshold, thereby proving that the difference in learning time is indeed statistically significant.

Results & Analysis

From the results of Question 4, we already know that there is no staistically significant change in the study time before and during COVID-19 for students from non low-income households. However, when we conducted t-test #1 to find out whether there was a significant difference between the study times for students from low-income and non low-income families before COVID-19, we found the difference to to be significant.

Next we conducted t-test #2 to find out if there was a significant statistical difference between the study times for low and non low-income families during COVID-19; this difference also turned out to be significant. Although, we already know that there was no significant change in the study time for non low-income families during COVID-19 and prior to it. As such, we cannot fully conclude if low income families are more impacted by COVID-19 in terms of education; more data analysis is required.

The purpose of conducting the two t-tests was to be able to compare the results of each analysis to see any changes in the values of the t-tests; the greater the t-value, the greater the stastical significance and the smaller the p-value, the greater the statistical significance.

The t-value produced by t-test #2 is smaller than that of t-test #1, the p-value for t-test #2 is greater than the p-value for t-test #1. As such, we can conclude the stastical significance has decreased from t-test #1 to t-test #2; this should have increased according to our hypothesis. Due to these findings, we have to accept the null hypothesis and reject our alternate hypothesis that COVID-19 had a more significant impact on the learning time of students from low-income families, as compared to non low-income families.

Data Limitations

In terms of the data, our limitations are the same as the ones mentioned in Questions 3 and 4. As mentioned before, our datasets are very limited due to the fact that usually data is only made available for any given year after the year has ended (or at the very end of the year as a sort of reflection). Also, COVID-19 has given rise to new issues and challenges; as such, data regarding those issues and challenges isn’t readily available. The datasets that we have used for Questions 3 and 4 are taken from the UK; while there are a lot of similarities between Canada and the UK, there are also many differences. All this being said, a limitation of our data is that it may not fully reflect the situation in Canada.
