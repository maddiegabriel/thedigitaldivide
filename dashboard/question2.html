Question 2

Does digital access impact student academic performance?


Question Background

In March 2020, the COVID-19 lockdown led to mass school closures all over the world. Millions of children were sent home to complete their school year online. A student’s ability to succeed in an online learning environment is impacted by many parameters, including their digital access. This dashboard examines the impact of a student's digital access on their academic performance.

Digital Access

is defined as having access to the Internet at home.

Academic Performance

is measured by mean NAEP test scores.

NAEP?

The National Assessment of Educational Progress is a standardized test measuring the proficiency of U.S. students in many academic subjects. Often called “The Nation’s Report Card”, it is administered by the U.S Department of Education.
The Data

We examined the 2019 NAEP results for Grade 4 and 8 students at the national level. The dataset being examined is composed of the average NAEP test scores on a scale from 0 to 500 in the subject areas of Reading and Mathematics. NAEP results are then reported for groups of students with similar characteristics (e.g. gender, race, home Internet access), not for individual students.

Methodology: Independent Samples T-Test

A t-test is a popular method of data analysis used to determine if the difference between two means is statistically significant. We used an independent samples t-test to compare the mean NAEP subject scores of students in two independent categories: those who have Internet access at home and those who do not. The response (dependent variable) is the students' mean NAEP test scores. We performed a separate t-test for each subject being studied: Mathematics and Reading.
Results: Math Scores
Interpretation

The p value for this t-test was very small (p-value < 2.2e-16). This means that, for the 2019 NAEP reading assessment administered to 4th-grade students, there is indeed a significant difference between the mean test scores of students who have access to the Internet at home and students who do not. On average, the students with access to the Internet at home performed significantly better academically than those who do not. In the plot to the left, the orange block is higher on the reading scale score; this means that the population category of students with access to the Internet at home has a higher mean test score. This method allowed us to determine that the observed difference in mean academic performance did not happen by chance; the difference is significant and could be used to prove that digital access has an impact on academic performance.

Findings

Going back to the original question, the results for this data analysis show that personal digital access can indeed impact student academic performance. An obvious limitation of these findings is that the data is for students in the U.S, not Canada. We used U.S. data because the U.S. has more standardized tests to measure academic performance than Canada. The largest limitation of this dataset is that it applies to a population of students in the U.S., however our project aims to examine the impact of COVID-19 on lower income Canadian families. While both countries are in North America, the education systems as well as levels of digital access for lower income citizens in Canada and the U.S. may vary significantly. Additionally, for this question, digital access is measured by having access to the Internet at home. In Question 1, it was measured as an aggregate value of Internet access at home and access to a personal smartphone, which is a slightly different metric. Lastly, this dataset has fairly unequal sampling between the two categories of students being assessed. There is a large difference in sample size between the sample size of students with digital access (approximately 90% of the population) and those without (approximately 10% of the population).
