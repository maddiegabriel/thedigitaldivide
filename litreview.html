Literature Review


A summary of ideas and findings from research in this field.



Digital Access & Digital Literacy

The digital divide is a commonly used term to describe the gap which exists between demographics who have access to modern technology, and those who do not (or who have restricted access). In the context of our project, this technology includes access to the internet and internet-enabled devices at home (personal digital access) as well as level of digital literacy in school-age children (K-12). The COVID-19 pandemic has resulted in the mass closure of schools across Canada and the world, sending many school children home to complete their education online. This phenomenon has the potential to widen the gap created by the digital divide.

Revisiting the Digital Divide in Canada: the Impact of Demographic Factors on Access to the Internet, Level of Online Activity, and Social Networking Site Usage.

Micheal explores digital divide in terms of people having access to the internet and the online activity generated by people of different classes, communities and social groups. Author also talks about the changing definition of digital divide. In earlier years digital divide was whether a person or family had a connection to the internet or not and the quality of the connection. Nowadays digital divide refers more to the online activity generated by the different people or the ability to generate a certain amount of digital activity. The author has divided the digital activity in two groups. One group is surfing the web for information or entertainment while the other group who leverages Social Networking Services (SNS). The author for most part has emphasized on the group leveraging SNS in his research. The author studies the digital divide of leveraging connectivity on SNS based on gender. “According to a 2013 Pew Internet and American Life report on social networking, women are no longer more likely to use SNSs than men in the United States (Duggan, 2013). The gap in SNS use by gender is closing from a difference of 15% (68% of women and 53% of men) in 2010 to just 4% (74% of women and 70% of men) in 2013, which is a result that is no longer statistically significant.”[1] It also explores people having an internet connection and being able to generate digital activity based on income levels and the level of education a person has.


COVID-19 and Digital Inequalities: Reciprocal Impacts and Mitigation Strategies.

Beaunoyer examines specifically the impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic on existing digital inequalities. She shows that a lack of digital access cuts people off from access to services, especially during lockdown. Beaunoyer discusses how education being even more dependent on digital access disproportionately affects the children of lower-income households. She states: “Limited access to digital technologies generally implies limited access to services, resources, and information – and their potential benefits – and an altered pattern of access to the other determinants of health (Golder, Newman, Biedrzycki, & Baum, 2010)”. Some gaps in this research exist. It assumes that lower-income households will have slower access to the internet and fewer internet enabled devices. It is also not specific to Canada and doesn’t consider how students being in class in places like Ontario changes the availability of digital access. Lastly, it makes some predictions about long term effects of COVID-19, which we won’t be able to validate until many years from now.


School Closures and the Online Preparedness of Children During the COVID-19 Pandemic.

In his study, Frenette provides an analysis of how the academic performance/learning quality of K-12 school children is impacted by the nation-wide closure of schools in Canada due to the pandemic. He discusses the necessity of student access to online resources and internet-connected devices, and how lower-income families might struggle with accessing these resources. This research estimates the percentage of Canadian households with children under the age of 18 who have steady access to these learning tools, as compared to their household income. This article finds that while most Canadian households with children under 18 have internet access at home, there are fewer internet-enabled devices per household member in lower-income households. This could pose a disadvantage to children of lower-income families who are forced to learn in a fully remote setting. Some gaps in this research exist; the article doesn’t differentiate between online preparedness in cities as compared to suburbs. While this article was published in April, a lot has changed since then; the pandemic situation is unraveling every day and we have since seen some students head back to school while others stay at home to learn virtually. This phenomenon might change some of the impacts of online preparedness.


Home Learning During Lockdown

Learning During the Lockdown: Real-Time Data on Children’s Experiences during Home Learning

In this source, Alison explores the amount of time students are spending in online learning during the COVID-19 pandemic correlated with household income. The online study includes 4000 parent participants of children aged 4–15 between Wednesday 29 April and Tuesday 12 May 2020. The obvious limitation to this data is that it will skew towards families who have a higher level of digital literacy and access to the internet. As a result it may leave out families at the bottom level of income and digital access. Further, this study took place in April 2020, and much about the learning environment has changed since then, which could also affect the data. The study suggested a positive correlation between the income of a household and the child(ren) of that household’s time spent online learning: “Children in the highest-income fifth of families spend 5.8 hours a day on educational activities, over 75 minutes more than their peers in the poorest fifth of households (4.5 hours).” It also suggested a positive correlation between household income and engagement of parent in student learning.


The Impact of ‘Learning at Home’ on the Educational Outcomes of Vulnerable Children in Australia during the COVID-19 Pandemic.

Drane’s research examines the impact of the COVID-19 school closures in terms of how moving to online delivery of education impacts those students who are the most socially and financially disadvantaged in society. Her paper asks: How can we support their continued learning through this unprecedented time? This study brings up many interesting perspectives on different issues which may impact academic success in a remote environment, including the increase in mental health issues for vulnerable students due to extra family and financial stressors during this already stressful time. In examining the lowest quintile of household income, the article finds that vulnerable students are more likely to be disengaged, which can lead to lower academic and social outcomes. Students are more inclined to experience issues related to digital access, including a steady high-speed internet connection and the required devices, which severely impacts the ability to succeed at online learning from home. Children from lower-income households are more likely to be exposed to inappropriate material and cyberbullying online. By using this source we are making the assumption that the experience of children from lower-income households in Australia is similar to that of children in Canada, and that the impact of COVID-19 school closures on both populations are comparable. Although this article focuses on a different population I still believe it is useful to our project as it makes many important connections between a child’s socioeconomic status and their educational impact due to COVID-19. This paper made us wonder about the impact of socioeconomic status on a Canadian child’s digital literacy; how does a lack of digital literacy impact the child’s ability to learn effectively in an online setting?


Parent Supporting Learning at Home During the COVID-19 Pandemic

Greenlee explores parental support for learning at home for children 14 years and older during the COVID-19 pandemic in a Canadian context. It was an online survey that took place from June 9 to 22, 2020, and involved over 32,000 parents or guardians. It explores multiple demographics and how they correlate specifically to internet access and online activity. There are major limitations to this study. A large proportion of the participants in the study were women who possessed a bachelor's degree or higher. Since the survey was online, it skews the participants towards those who have digital access, omitting the most vulnerable groups. As a result, the findings reflect only the responses of those who completed the questionnaire and “cannot be generalized to the entire Canadian population.”[1] The findings indicate that the level of education of the parents had a positive correlation with the amount of structured academic activities their child participated in at home. In addition, it also found that the parents' level of education had a positive correlation with their level of concern in their child’s academic success.
